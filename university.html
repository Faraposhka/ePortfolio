I3: ABMobility

Spatial inequalities have been long studied, particularly within the domains of sociology and urban studies. In Austria, such a disparity is evidently highlighted through mobility patterns between rural and urbanised areas, which forms a major policy challenge within the current European and Austrian climate targets and is further complicated by ongoing demographic trends. At the same time, since COVID-19 pandemic Austria has faced an increasing trend of counter-urbanization, with citizens moving towards peripheral and rural areas where access to public transportation has been limited.

This project addresses the existing methodological gap through development of a modelling approach that integrates the qualitative data of agents (citizens) with the quantitative opportunities that are presented by agent-based modelling. It is argued that through understanding of relevant concepts (associations) to commuters, a more accurate model could be established to evaluate existing infrastructure and implement policies aimed at a modal shift in mobility.

SDI Services Implementation

The purpose of the project is to provide information about current school infrastructure (school as providers of educational services) and estimate shortcomings or opportunities for municipalities. For instance, exemplary usage could be as follows: user looks at a municipality, sees that it usually takes 2 hours on average to commute to schools. After that possible solutions could include either placing a new school (if other municipalities have also high commuting times) or increasing accessibility of public transport.

Project should eventually present information on spatial distribution of schools and their accessibility to help take informed actions for improving commutes to school or built schools where educational infrastructure gaps currently exist.

Application Development

Despite the growing interest in data-driven urban planning, there is a lack of publicly available tools for analyzing real estate dynamics in Moscow and lack of open data which could be provided for research objectives.

The prototype web application reveals quite a lot of potential benefits of using Python for exploring tendencies in Moscow real-estate market at different spatial resolutions (e.g., data could be aggregated not only at municipal level, but also through grids or other spatial representations; moreover, a scalability option is present: data could be obtained for different regions of Russia across different time periods). However, there are some limitations regarding software and data itself.

Spatial Simulation

This final project "Bicycle Street Design" was a collaborative effort between Aleksandr Faraponov and Lisa Glahsl. The objective was to simulate interactions between cyclists and pedestrians, with a primary focus on cyclists. Throughout the course, the model was continuously refined based on both the computational capabilities of the GAMA platform and the students’ ability to implement new functionalities. These iterative improvements will be discussed in the Discussion section.

From a broader perspective, the model successfully captures pedestrian-cyclist interactions, leveraging a grid-based approach with action areas to define behavioral zones — (probably) an advantage over purely graph-based models. However, certain refinements are needed to enhance predictive accuracy, resolve performance and optimisation issues (the model runs really slow with 10 pedestrian agents or more).

Advanced Cartography

Since the collapse of communist regimes in Europe and the fall of the “iron curtain”, new countries started slowly joining the new European Union (European Community before 1993).

East Germany was to be the first with the German reunification in 1990. Czechia, Slovakia, Slovenia and Hungary joined it more than a decade later, in 2004. The last ex-socialist countries along the “iron curtain” were Romania und Bulgaria, who entried the EU in 2007. Croatia joined the latest, in 2013.

This project tries to explore if a significant economic disparity still persists between neighboring NUTS-2 regions along the former “iron curtain”. It is achieved through the analysis of economic productivity. All the measurements for productivity are presented as euros per hour worked.
